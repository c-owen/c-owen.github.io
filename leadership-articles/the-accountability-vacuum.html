The Accountability Vacuum

You get promoted to lead a team. Your manager keeps attending the strategic meetings and keeps the executive relationships, and hands you the execution. You're expected to make informed decisions without the context that would make them informed, and when things go wrong, which they will, you're the convenient place for the blame to land. I call this the accountability vacuum: responsibility flows downward while authority and information stay concentrated at the top.

To be clear, I'm not talking about legitimate constraints on authority. In regulated industries or genuinely sensitive situations, no manager can hand over unlimited decision-making power, and that's fine. The dysfunction I'm describing is being held accountable without clear boundaries, without the necessary context, and without any way to escalate decisions that sit beyond your scope.

The mechanics are simple. Your manager sits in the rooms where strategy gets set and receives the executive communications, while you operate on incomplete information, responsible for outcomes you can't fully see or influence. Success and failure then get treated very differently. When the delegated work succeeds, it demonstrates your manager's effective delegation. When it fails, it demonstrates your poor execution. Heads I win, tails you lose. Even well-meaning managers create this imbalance when they aren't deliberate about how they recognize outcomes, and the effect is that the manager never faces consequences for a broken delegation practice.

Why do managers do this? Some are afraid of losing relevance or control, and keeping the strategic relationships to themselves preserves a sense of indispensability while offloading the hard work. Sometimes the pressure comes from above: in a crisis, upper management leans on middle managers to make things happen without providing new resources, and the impossible directive gets passed down intact. And plenty of organizations simply never notice that authority and responsibility have to travel together, because they think of delegation as distributing workload rather than distributing power.

What happens next is predictable. Projects suffer, because the person running them can't make the decisions the project needs. High performers recognize a setup when they see one, and they either burn out trying to succeed anyway or leave for an organization that pairs authority with responsibility. And because the blame lands on the person instead of the process, the organization learns nothing from the failure and runs the same play again with someone else.

Breaking the cycle means treating delegation as a transfer of decision-making power, not just tasks. If someone is accountable for outcomes, they need visibility into the strategic context that shapes those outcomes: a seat in the relevant meetings, the rationale behind decisions that affect their work, and clear boundaries around what they do and don't control. Above all, the manager who delegates stays accountable for the quality of the delegation itself. When delegated work fails, the first question should be whether the person had what they needed to succeed, and only then whether they executed well.

If you're currently inside the vacuum, your best defense is documentation and explicit boundary-setting. Write down every directive, every information gap, and every resource request that goes unanswered. When you're handed a new responsibility, ask immediately what authority comes with it and what success looks like. Escalate constraints early, before problems compound, and be specific about the factors you can't influence and how they affect delivery. And resist the urge to treat quietly absorbing all of this as dedication. It isn't. It enables a dysfunctional system, and it will eventually cost you both the project and your career.
