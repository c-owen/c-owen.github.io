Everything Is Everyone's Responsibility

This week I opened a meeting by telling a room full of capable people that our lab was disgusting. I didn't dress it up, because the situation didn't deserve dressing up: years of accumulated personal clutter, stacks of papers belonging to no one anyone could name, computers parked wherever a computer would fit, equipment halls you could no longer walk through. Nobody in that room was learning anything from me. The state of the lab was a fact everyone had been stepping around, literally, for a long time. Sometimes the most useful thing a leader can do is say the obvious thing plainly, so that it stops being ambient and becomes a subject.

What happened next was the interesting part. Every single person, immediately and without hesitation, reached for a reason the lab couldn't be cleaned up. The equipment might belong to a project that still needed it. The papers were somebody's records. Nobody had the authority to throw away another person's things. It wasn't clear whose time it should come out of, or whose budget, or whose call it was. And every one of those reasons was individually true. But sitting there listening, I realized the room was answering a question I hadn't asked out loud: how did it get this way? I was watching the mechanism operate in real time. Fifteen locally reasonable objections, running for years, compound into a hallway you can't walk through.

I want to be precise about what I'm claiming, because this is not a story about my team being unusually bad. These are people I trust with genuinely hard problems, and the reflex I watched is a human reflex, one I've felt plenty of times myself. Psychologists have a name for it: diffusion of responsibility. The more people who share a problem, the less it belongs to any one of them. Put twenty conscientious professionals around a shared mess and you can get less action than you'd get from one person alone, because each of them is reasonably waiting for whoever's job it is. The trap is that a shared space, like most of the important things a team owns together, is precisely the thing that is nobody's job. Which means it's everybody's.

Every team has a lab. In software it's the flaky test everyone retries and no one fixes, the onboarding doc three hires out of date, the shared drive nobody can find anything in, the alert channel everyone has muted. Ask why any of these stays broken and you'll hear the same phrase I heard in that meeting, in one form or another: it's technically someone else's job. I'll say what I believe about that sentence, which is that in a professional environment it is indefensible. Job descriptions exist and they matter, but a job description is a floor, not a fence. Teams exist in the first place because the work that matters refuses to fit inside the boxes we draw; the gaps between the boxes belong to all of us, and tending them is part of the job.

What people are actually waiting for, I think, is rarely a decision. It's permission. Someone else to be responsible, someone else to take the first step, someone else to say the mold can be broken. And the uncomfortable truth is that there is no permission slip waiting around the corner, no one coming to tap you on the shoulder and say: hey, you're free to get started on the hard thing now. It's you, it's me, it's us. We're all there is. Anyone in that room could have moved first at any point in the last several years, and that is exactly why nobody did.

The principle needs its boundaries stated, or it curdles into something worse. Everything being everyone's responsibility is not a license to freelance in a domain you don't understand, and not an excuse for management to point at culture instead of staffing a chronic gap. The same "everyone's job" landing on the same two volunteers every time is a quiet tax on your most conscientious people, and fixing that is a management problem with a management solution. The principle is narrower and harder than the slogan: when you see a problem that belongs to no one, you don't get to un-see it by reciting your job title. You can hand it off, you can escalate it, you can schedule it. What you can't do is decide it isn't yours.

The lab will get cleaned up, and it won't be because the objections got answered one by one or a permission slip finally arrived. It'll happen the way these things always happen: a few people deciding to act as if it were their job, and everyone else discovering it had been theirs all along too. The first trash bag is the permission slip. If you're standing in a room where everyone is explaining why the hard thing can't be started, notice that the room already has plenty of reasons. What it's missing is a first mover, and you're as qualified as anyone in it.
