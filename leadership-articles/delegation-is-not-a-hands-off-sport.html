Delegation Is Not a Hands-Off Sport

There's a version of delegation where a manager hands off a piece of work, frames the handoff as trust, and then backs away so completely that the person is left without the context or support they need. Whatever the manager's intention, that isn't useful delegation. It's much closer to abdication.

Most managers who fall into this pattern aren't trying to leave anyone stranded. They may be busy, or they may genuinely believe that staying out of the way is how people grow. The problem is that the person receiving the work often doesn't know what the manager knows: why earlier decisions were made, which constraints really matter, who needs to be involved, or where resistance is likely to come from. Sometimes a ten-minute conversation would prevent several days of work in the wrong direction, but the organizational knowledge that could clear the path remains unused in the manager's head.

A lack of direction also tends to come with a lack of useful feedback. If a manager isn't close enough to understand how the work is progressing, they probably won't notice a misunderstanding while it is still easy to correct. By the time they become involved again, the options may be narrower and the correction more expensive. The person who took on the assignment is then left dealing with consequences that could have been avoided with a little more attention earlier.

Over time, this changes what people are willing to take on. A stretch assignment stops looking like an opportunity to grow and starts looking like a risk they will have to carry alone. Some people become reluctant to volunteer. Others make increasingly conservative decisions or start asking for approval at every turn. Managers can misread those behaviors as a lack of initiative without recognizing how their own pattern of engagement helped create them.

The answer isn't constant involvement, and it certainly isn't second-guessing every decision. Good delegation begins with a shared understanding of the outcome, the boundaries of the assignment, and the decisions the person can make independently. From there, the manager needs to remain accessible and engaged enough to recognize when guidance would help. How much involvement that requires depends on the person, the work, and the consequences of getting it wrong.

Managers often tell their teams, “You can always come to me,” but accessibility is demonstrated rather than announced. People notice what happens when they ask a question, raise a concern, or admit that they are unsure. If those moments are met with impatience—or if the manager only reappears when something has gone wrong—the invitation stops meaning very much.

Management is a hard job, and it is supposed to be. Much of the difficulty lies in knowing when to step in, when to coach, and when to let someone work through a problem without taking it back from them. Delegating the work does not remove the manager's responsibility to give the person a fair chance of succeeding. The goal is to step back far enough for someone else to take ownership without stepping so far away that they are left to manage the risk alone.
